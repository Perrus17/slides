Skills, jobs and regional coherence

Measuring skill relatedness and specialization using
information from two million job postings

by Martin Henning, Petrus Garefelt,
Hanna Martin and Zoltán Elekes

Swedish Labour Mobility Lab

The overall aim of the Swedish Labour Mobility Lab is to combine unique contemporary datasets
and historical insights to map the labour mobility structures into expanding jobs in Sweden,
and increase the understanding of the ways in which skills, gender norms and geography
affect who enters expanding jobs, in which ways, and under which working conditions

Background and aim

In recent years, researchers have developed a number of strategies, including a variety of relatedness measures, to approach new questions around regional economic coherence and regional path dependency (Hidalgo et al., 2018).

A stream of studies by now reveal that regions are more likely to branch into related activities (creating path dependency), and that activities that are less related to the technological core of regions are more likely to exit the local economy (Boschma 2017)









(1) division of labour is spatial











(2) economic activities concentrate in space













(3) related activities jointly concentrate in a coherent manner, and this coherence arises because of a regional path dependent selection process (Neffke et al., 2011)

While the ideas around skills and jobs have grown into a huge geographical literature, most regional researchers still, for practical reasons, had to rely on skills as an unobservable and aggregate entity.

In its extreme, this turns into a validity issue – when it comes to what kind of qualitative skills are actually captured in the measures, and what this tells us about spatial heterogeneity among skill categories.

The issue is not trivial or unimportant, however.

First, there is a need to empirically validate some of the fundamental assumptions of the evolutionary labour mobility literature Second, there is a strong need for measures that make concrete skills observable, or at least observable by close representation tokens. Third, regional coherence and path dependency need to be demonstrated by a precise measure

This paper aims to give some answers to these challenges

Research questions

Which activities are related to each other in the economy,
and does this relatedness matter for

(1) how people switch between jobs,
(2) how people are compensated for their strategies when they change jobs and,
ultimately, for (3) how particular types of jobs are concentrated in space and evolve over time?

Theory

Data and empirical strategy



We use open data on job postings and enrichment methods made available by the
Swedish Public Employment Service

The basis for skill extraction is the about 6 million job postings in the database Historical Jobs between the years 2006-2021, covering all job postings published on the Swedish Public Employment Service's job advertisement platform (Platsbanken) during these years.

We define jobs as cross-combinations of occupations (3-digit) and industries (2-digit) in the standard classification systems

Figure 1 gives a schematic description of the approach.

First, this dataset has entered the Job Enrichment API (shadowed area in the figure). Here, the dataset of ads first enters into a Pre-processing stage, where raw texts are prepared for analysis. This renders an Embedding layer which represents an efficient method to search for and retrieve words that represent skills, given the training of the model on all 6 million job postings.
Second, we select and merge three annual sets of ads, detailing over 1.7 million advertised jobs 2019-2021. We communicate this set to the servers of the Embedding Layer, which extract words that, given their linguistic context, are likely to be representations of skill descriptions








This is our Enriched dataset ->

However, it is of course very difficult to calculate job relatedness based on these text lists, and they also vary considerable in size.

We therefore feed the lists of skills into a feature vector representation model (Doc2Vec, Le and Mikolov 2014). We measure the degree to which jobs relate to each other by calculating the cosine similarity between the vectors produced by the learning algorithm
