Skills, jobs and regional coherence

Measuring skill relatedness and specialization using information from two million job postings

by Martin Henning, Petrus Garefelt,
Hanna Martin and Zoltán Elekes

This research was funded by FORTE through the

Swedish Labour Mobility Lab

Background and aim

In recent years, researchers have developed a number of strategies, including a variety of relatedness measures, to approach new questions around regional economic coherence and regional path dependency (Hidalgo et al., 2018).

Lots of relatedness measurements

• Entropy-based (Frenken et al., 2007; Boschma et al., 2008; Wixe & Andersson 2017)

• Co-location (Muneepeerakul et al., 2013; Farinha et al., 2019

• Labour flow (Neffke & Henning, 2013)


Some key challenges

• Industries or occupations measured as isolated units

• High risk of circular reasoning using flow-based indicators

• Hard to measure skills and human capital and their role for coherence

BUT new data, new machine learning measures...


Research questions

Which activities are related to each other in the economy, and does this relatedness matter for (1) how people switch between jobs, (2) how people are compensated for their strategies when they change jobs, and for (3) how particular types of jobs are concentrated in space?

Data and empirical strategy

We use open data from more than two million recent Swedish job postings combined by machine learning methods

• What types of skills that are demanded for jobs in the economy

• Which jobs are more similar in terms of the skills that they require.


Our approach


(1) Pre-processing -> Embedding layer (fastText)

(2) Fuzzy-matching -> Classification (Ind/Occ combinations)

(3) Skill-tokens -> Feature vector representation (Doc2Vec, Le and Mikolov 2014)

Result: flows

Previous assumptions about the foundations of labour-based relatedness measures in terms of flow directions and wage premiums make sense on average

Result: wages

Related mobility is more advantageous to some labour groups, than to others

Result: recruitment strategy (more focused is better, given regional structure)

Regional economic portfolios are on average coherent, but this coherence is negatively linked to regional wealth









Conclusions


(1) a new job relatedness measure

(2) coherence (sharing)

(3) values of clusters within cities (establishing a credible labour market for certain specialists

(4) wage competition (which benefits workers
